## Overview
'Inaba shidare' is widely considered the "powerhouse" of the red weeping dissectums. While 'Crimson Queen' is prized for her delicate refinement, 'Inaba shidare' is valued for its sheer vigor, its massive cascading form, and its extraordinary ability to handle environmental stress. It features much larger, bolder lobes than other lace-leafs, which allow it to absorb more sunlight and grow significantly faster. If you want a weeping red maple that can "hold its own" in a large landscape or a sunny location, this is the cultivar of choice.

## 1. Cultural Context & Historical Anecdotes
The name 'Inaba shidare' carries a historical weight. *Inaba* refers to an ancient province in Japan (now part of the Tottori Prefecture), while *shidare* means "weeping" or "cascading." It has been documented for over **150 years** and was one of the first red weepers to be successfully mass-produced for the Western world.

Historically, it was selected not just for its color, but for its "muscularity." Collectors in the 19th century noticed that this particular seedling grew twice as fast as other red dissectums and was less prone to the "branch-tip die-back" that plagued more delicate varieties. It proved that a lace-leaf could be a "real tree" for a "real garden."

## 2. Seasonal Transitions: Layer by Layer
'Inaba shidare' offers a high-saturation, dramatic color arc:

- **Spring Emergence**: The leaves arrive in early April as a deep, saturated carmine-purple. They are larger than most dissectum leaves, looking like broad, feathery hands. The new wood is also a dark reddish-purple, making the entire tree look like a dark, violet cloud.
- **Summer Maturation**: Throughout the heat of summer, 'Inaba shidare' is a color champion. It maintains a deep, dark purple-burgundy (almost black in some lights) even in the 90Â°F sun of July. Because the leaves are thicker than 'Crimson Queen', they have a glossy, waxy sheen that resists "bleaching."
- **Autumn Finale**: The fall transition is a dramatic shift from dark purple to a brilliant, burning scarlet. Because the tree is such a vigorous grower, it produces a massive amount of foliage, meaning the fall show is an overwhelming wall of red.
- **Winter Silhouette**: The branching habit is a powerful, arching "mushroom" shape. The wood is thicker and darker than other weepers, providing a strong structural presence in the winter. It holds heavy snow better than more delicate varieties because its branches are "flexible" and "strong" rather than "brittle."

## 3. Design Use & Companion Planting
### Landscape Role
- **Slope Coverage**: This is the best tree for a steep embankment or a large retaining wall. Its branches "pour" over the edge like a purple waterfall.
- **The Large Focal Point**: Because it can reach a spread of 12-15 feet, it is a "legacy tree." Plant it in the center of a large lawn or as a standalone specimen in a grand entryway.

### Companion Planting & Understory
- **Gold & Purple Duel**: Pair with gold-leaved cultivars like **'Summer Gold'** or yellow-blooming perennials like **Coreopsis**. The deep purple of the 'Inaba shidare' and the bright gold/yellow create a high-contrast palette that is visually arresting.
- **Silver Background**: Plant near a silver-leaved **Conifer** like *Picea pungens* 'Hoopsii' (Blue Spruce). The dark purple lace-leaf set against the icy blue-silver of the spruce is a color combination used by professional estate designers.
- **Groundcover**: A base of **Yellow Sedum** ('Angelina') or **Acorus gramineus** 'Ogon' provides a bright "carpet" that highlights the dark cascading branches hovering just inches above.

## 4. Advanced Horticultural Notes
- **Lobe Width as an Advantage**: Horticulturally, 'Inaba shidare' is successful because its individual lobes are wider than other dissectums. This increased surface area means more photosynthesis, which translates directly to faster trunk caliper development and better winter hardiness.
- **Grafting Heights**: This cultivar is almost always "top-worked" (grafted at 3-4 feet) to allow its branches room to weep. If you buy a "low-grafted" one, you will need to stake a central leader upward for several years to create the "umbrella" effect.
- **Nutrient Efficiency**: It is a "heavy drinker" compared to other dwarfs. It needs deep, moisture-retentive soil but is more tolerant of a wider pH range (5.5 to 7.2) than more delicate variegated types.

## 5. Troubleshooting & Variety-Specific Issues
- **Bottom-Heavy Growth**: Because it is so vigorous, 'Inaba shidare' can become "bottom-heavy," with the lower branches growing much faster than the top. 
    - *Action*: Use "thinning" cuts in summer to reduce the weight of the lower skirt. This redirects energy back to the top of the canopy and prevents the tree from becoming a flat "pancake."
- **Weight Management**: In old age, the main structural branches can become very heavy with foliage. 
    - *Action*: Inspect the main crotch of the tree for splitting or stress. Selective thinning of the canopy every 3 years is required to maintain the tree's health and prevent branch failure in high winds.
- **Sun Tolerance Limits**: While "high" for a dissectum, it still has limits. In Zone 9, the dark purple leaves absorb a lot of heat. If the leaves turn "bronze" and then "grey," the tree is literally "cooking." 
    - *Action*: Increase mulch depth to 4 inches (keeping it away from the trunk) to keep the root zone cool.

> [!TIP]
> If you have a 'Crimson Queen' that is struggling and looks thin, replace it with an 'Inaba shidare'. It is the "Plan B" that almost always succeeds where other red weepers fail.
